# Guide: Building a LinkedIn Profile That Converts

## The Principle

Your profile is not a resume. It's a recognition engine.

Every element serves one function: make the right person stop and think "this person is from my world" within 100-200 milliseconds. The wrong person should scroll past without noticing. That's not a failure - that's the design.

---

## The House Analogy

Your profile is a house. Visitors decide whether to enter based on what they see from the street.

| Profile Element | House Equivalent | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Banner | Paint job / exterior | Tribe declaration. Pre-verbal recognition. |
| Headline | Front door sign | Identity stack. Makes the right person stop. |
| Profile photo | Person at the door | Carrier Trust signal. |
| About section | Walking inside | World paragraph. All five language layers. |
| Featured section | Living room display | Proof. Lead magnets. Best tribal content. |
| Experience | Foundation | Credibility infrastructure. |

---

## 1. Banner

**This is your most pre-verbal piece of real estate.** It fires before the headline, before the about section, before any words register.

### What works
- An image that encodes your tribe's world. The tribe recognises it before they've read a single word.
- Text overlay with tribal positioning (not a generic tagline).

### What doesn't work
- Stock image of a city skyline
- Corporate headshot collage
- Generic gradient with your name on it
- Anything that could belong to anyone in any industry

### The test
Show your banner to someone in your tribe with no other context. Do they recognise their world? If yes - keep it. If they see "a nice professional banner" - redo it.

### Specs
- 1584 x 396 pixels
- Keep key text/imagery in the centre - edges get cropped on mobile
- The banner + headline are seen together. Design them as a unit.

---

## 2. Headline

**Under 70 characters for mobile visibility.** 220 characters max. This is your identity stack compressed.

### The Rule
Do NOT start with "I help."

"I help [X] do [Y]" is the most generic positioning structure on LinkedIn. It describes your service. It does not describe their world. The entire point is to describe the world first.

### Three Formats That Work

**Format 1: Tribal identity + what they get**
```
Commercial Strategy for Ag-Facing Businesses | Trusted by Producers
```

**Format 2: Tribal pain point + your position**
```
Engineers are underpaid (except for mine)
```

**Format 3: Their felt experience in one line**
```
Business owners putting in the hours but operating at 60%
```

### What makes these work
- Insider language ("ag-facing," "producers," "PE," "operating at 60%")
- Describes the tribe's world or pain, not your method
- The wrong person reads it and thinks "not me" - that's correct
- The right person reads it and feels something shift

### Headline Diagnostic
- Does it use at least one word that passes the five-word test (only insiders understand)?
- Would the right person stop on this before consciously processing why?
- Does it fail for outsiders? (If everyone nods, it's too broad.)

---

## 3. Profile Photo

The Carrier Trust signal. Not about looking "professional." About looking like someone from the tribe's world.

### Guidelines
- Clear face, good lighting, recent
- Context matters more than polish
- If your tribe is corporate - look corporate. If your tribe is agricultural - look agricultural. Match the world.
- Avoid: sunglasses, group photos cropped, logos instead of faces, photos from 10 years ago

---

## 4. About Section

This is where the world paragraph lives. The visitor clicked through - now you need to prove you're from inside their world, not observing it from outside.

### Structure (in order)

**Opening: The World Paragraph (6-10 sentences)**
First person. Present tense. Describes your ideal client's week from inside their experience. All five language layers:

1. Functional - what they actually do all day
2. Technical - insider vocabulary (must pass leakage test)
3. Cultural - what their world feels like
4. Psychic - their internal monologue, the 11pm thought
5. Narrative - the recurring loop/pattern

**Test:** Show it cold to an ideal client. If they think you're describing them - it's working.

**Middle: Your Director's Cut credentials**
Not a resume. The scars that prove you've been inside the world.

- Specific lived experience inside the tribe (not credentials - scars)
- "I've sat in the room when the operating model collapsed and watched five executives blame each other for the same problem"
- This is Carrier Trust. The K factor. It can't be faked.

**Close: What happens when they work with you**
- Specific outcomes, not vague promises
- Named results from real situations
- One clear next step (DM keyword, link in featured, booking link in comments)

### About Section Anti-Patterns
- Starting with "I am a passionate..." (nobody cares)
- Listing credentials without tribal context
- Writing in third person
- Generic value propositions ("I help leaders unlock their potential")
- Anything that could be copy-pasted onto a competitor's profile and still make sense

## 5. Featured Section

Your living room display. Three slots maximum. Each one should do a specific job.

### Slot 1: Lead Magnet or Diagnostic
The thing someone DMs you a keyword to receive. Directly connected to your tribe's core pain. Named with tribal vocabulary.

### Slot 2: Best Tribal Authority Post
Your highest-performing post where the comments extend the conversation in insider language. Social proof that the tribe recognises you.

### Slot 3: Case Study or Proof
A specific result. Named situation (anonymised if needed). Before/after. The kind of detail that makes the tribe think "that's exactly my problem."

### What NOT to feature
- Generic "about me" videos
- Press mentions nobody in your tribe cares about
- Content that scored in the Community Trap or Noise quadrants

---

## 6. Experience Section

Most people treat this as a resume. It's actually credibility infrastructure for Carrier Trust.

### For each role, answer:
- What tribe was I inside during this role?
- What tribal vocabulary can I use to describe what I did?
- What specific outcomes did I produce that my current tribe would recognise?

### Write experience descriptions in tribal language
- Not: "Led cross-functional transformation initiatives for enterprise clients"
- Instead: "Rebuilt the operating model for a 400-person company where the CFO, CRO, and CPO were running three different strategies. Aligned them to one mission in 90 days. Revenue grew 23% in the next two quarters."

The experience section is where the tribe validates that you've actually been in the rooms you claim to have been in.

---

## 7. Skills & Endorsements

Low priority but not zero. Ensure your top 3 pinned skills match your tribal positioning. Remove generic skills that dilute ("Microsoft Office," "Team Management"). Keep only skills that reinforce your identity stack.

---

## The Profile Conversion Flow

Here's how a lead actually moves through your profile:

```
Content in feed (tribal recognition fires)
    |
Click profile (banner + headline = second recognition check)
    |
Scan about section (world paragraph = "this person knows my life")
    |
Check featured (proof + lead magnet = "I want that")
    |
DM or connection request
```

Every step is a recognition gate. If any element breaks the tribal signal - generic banner, "I help" headline, brochure about section - the visitor drops off.

## The Meaning Score Applied to Profile Elements

Score every visible text element:

**MS = TD x BA**

| Element | Low Score Example | High Score Example |
|---|---|---|
| Headline | "Leadership Consultant \| Helping Teams Thrive" (MS: 0.8) | "Your team stuck in daily noise? I fix the operating model" (MS: 7.2) |
| Banner text | "Strategy \| Leadership \| Growth" (MS: 0.3) | "Operating Model Consultant for [tribal identifier]" (MS: 8.0) |
| About opener | "I am a passionate consultant with 15 years of experience..." (MS: 0.2) | World paragraph in first person describing their week (MS: 9.0) |
| Featured | Generic PDF: "5 Tips for Better Leadership" (MS: 0.5) | "The Operating Model Diagnostic - starts with one question your leadership team can't answer" (MS: 8.5) |

Kill anything scoring below 3.

- [ ] Encodes the tribe's world visually

### Headline
- [ ] Under 70 characters (mobile-visible)
- [ ] Does NOT start with "I help"
- [ ] Uses at least one word that passes the five-word test
- [ ] Describes their world or pain, not your service
- [ ] The right person stops. The wrong person scrolls. Both are correct.

### About Section
- [ ] Opens with a world paragraph (first person, present tense, all 5 language layers)
- [ ] Contains Director's Cut credentials (scars, not certificates)
- [ ] Uses tribal vocabulary without explaining it
- [ ] Ends with a clear next step
- [ ] Could NOT be copy-pasted onto a competitor's profile

### Featured
- [ ] Lead magnet or diagnostic named with tribal vocabulary
- [ ] Best tribal authority post (comments prove it)
- [ ] Specific proof/case study

### Experience
- [ ] Written in tribal language, not resume language
- [ ] Specific outcomes the tribe would recognise
- [ ] Reinforces Carrier Trust - proves you've been in the rooms

### Overall
- [ ] Every text element scores 3+ on the Meaning Score
- [ ] Profile reads as "someone from inside the world" not "someone selling to the world"
- [ ] A tribe member visiting cold would think "this person gets my life" within 10 seconds
