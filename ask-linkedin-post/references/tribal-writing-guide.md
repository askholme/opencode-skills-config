# Writing Guide: My Tribe Stack

Operating model consultant for companies that scaled faster than their structure could handle.

---

## My Tribe Definition

### Who they are
Leaders (CEO, COO, VP Ops, CFO, CHRO) at companies between 100-1000 people where the operating model that worked at 50 broke somewhere around 150-300 and nobody rebuilt it. They just kept adding people, tools, and workarounds.

### The professional circumstance (primary tribe)
The company grew. The structure didn't. Now everything runs on heroics, tribal knowledge, and firefighting. They've tried reorgs. They've tried new tools. They've tried "alignment workshops." Nothing stuck because the problem isn't people or tools - it's the operating model underneath.

### What makes this a real tribe (six rooms check)

**Room 1 - Scenes (not vocabulary):** This tribe doesn't have exclusive words. "Decision rights" and "RACI" leak into every MBA classroom and consulting deck. The tribal signal is not a word - it's a micro-scene. A one-sentence situation that only someone who's lived inside a broken operating model would recognise. "Only Peter knows how this is done" is not jargon. It's a moment. And every person in this tribe has met Peter.

**Room 2 - Entitativity:** These leaders recognise each other instantly. At any conference, two COOs from scaling companies find each other in 10 minutes and start comparing war stories.

**Room 3 - Shared enemy:** Management consultants who deliver 200-slide decks and leave. Reorgs that change boxes on a chart but not behaviour. The CEO who thinks "alignment" means "everyone agrees with me." Tools sold as solutions to structural problems.

**Room 4 - Initiation cost:** You don't understand this world until you've lived through at least one failed transformation. Years of professional pain. The cost is real.

**Room 5 - Sacred values:** Simplicity over complexity. Decisions over consensus. Clarity over comprehensiveness. Don't reorganise what you haven't diagnosed.

**Room 6 - Kitchen counter:** AI transformation pressure, post-M&A integration chaos, RTO friction breaking operating rhythms, cost-cutting mandates hitting companies that never had cost discipline in the first place.

## How This Tribe's Recognition Works

### The language problem
Standard tribal positioning assumes the hook is a word that fires recognition in 100-200ms. One word. Instant. Like "Fran" for CrossFitters.

This tribe doesn't have that. The C-level meeting room uses the same words as a business school. "Decision rights," "governance," "alignment" - they all leak. Any MBA grad, any Scrum Master, any middle manager who read one HBR article uses the same vocabulary.

Going industry-deep to find real vocabulary (logistics: "bill of lading," "drayage") would create exclusive words but shrink the tribe to irrelevance. The tribe cuts across industries. The COO at a SaaS company and the COO at a manufacturing company recognise the same scenes. They don't share industry vocabulary. They share operational suffering.

### The scene solution
This tribe's recognition mechanism is **situational, not lexical.**

A micro-scene in one sentence fires the same neurological recognition as a tribal word - just in 2-3 seconds instead of 200ms. When it hits, it hits harder. The reader isn't recognising a word. They're recognising their own Tuesday morning.

**The test changes accordingly:**
- Original five-word test: Can you name 5 words only insiders understand?
- This tribe's version: Can you describe 5 situations in one sentence that only insiders have lived through?

### What this means for content
- **Hooks are always a scene, not a concept.** Not "alignment matters." Instead: "The CEO contradicted last week's priority and everyone just wrote it down."
- **The scene IS the psychic language.** In vocabulary-rich tribes, psychic language is separate from technical language. Here, the scene and the 11pm thought are the same thing. "Only Peter knows how this is done" is simultaneously a functional description and an internal scream.
- **Technical vocabulary is supporting, not leading.** Use terms like "decision rights" or "operating rhythm" mid-post for structural credibility. Never as the hook. They don't fire recognition.
- **Scenes don't leak.** "RACI" leaks everywhere. "We need this done in the system before lunch today" does not. Nobody outside the broken-operating-model world recognises that specific flavour of panic.

## How to Use the Scene Bank

### One scene = one post
Don't combine three scenes into one post. Each one is specific enough to fire recognition on its own. Combining dilutes the "holy shit that's my company" moment.

### The scene IS the hook
These scenes are psychic language - the exact situations people live through inside the world. Lead with them. Don't paraphrase. Don't clean them up. The messiness is the signal.

### The structural root IS the reframe
The post moves from "here's what you're experiencing" (recognition) to "here's what's actually broken" (authority) to "here's what to do about it" (brand alignment).

### Each scene generates 5 posts

1. **The scene as a standalone recognition post** - Template 1
2. **The structural root as a diagnostic post** - Template 3
3. **The narrative loop the scene sits inside** - Template 2
4. **A case study of fixing that specific scene** (anonymised) - Template 4
5. **A contrast between what the company tried vs what actually worked** - Template 3

18 scenes x 5 posts = 90 posts. Five months of content before you need new material.

## The Content Layers

### Layer 1: Scenes (replaces "functional" and "psychic" - they merge in this tribe)

The scene bank above. These are simultaneously what happens (functional) and what it feels like (psychic). "Jan is out sick for a week, invoicing breaks down" is both a description of Tuesday and an 11pm anxiety.

**Use for:** Hooks. Always. Every post opens with a scene.

### Layer 2: Technical vocabulary (supporting role only)

- Decision rights
- Operating rhythm / governance cadence
- Run/change split
- Process ownership
- Escalation framework
- Intake process
- Capacity planning
- Communication architecture

**Use for:** Mid-post credibility. The structural diagnosis section. Never as the hook - these words don't fire recognition. They signal expertise after recognition has already fired.

**Rule:** If you're explaining what any of these terms mean, you're writing for the wrong audience. Use them. Don't define them.

### Layer 3: Cultural texture (what their world feels like)

- The specific exhaustion of being the person who "holds it all together"
- The politics of a leadership team where everyone is smart and nobody agrees
- The feeling of presenting a reorg slide deck knowing it won't survive first contact with reality
- The unspoken rule that you don't challenge the CEO's pet project
- The resignation of watching a new initiative launch knowing it'll be abandoned in 8 weeks
- The loneliness of being the only person in the room who sees the structural problem while everyone else debates symptoms
- The quiet dread of the Monday morning leadership meeting
- The Saturday morning text that confirms you are the backup plan for everything
- The exhaustion of motivating people toward goals that changed last week
- The anxiety of not knowing which version of the CEO you'll get today
- The frustration of watching people avoid decisions by pushing them upward
- The helplessness of knowing the SOP exists and knowing nobody will open it
- The slow realisation that problems are rotting in silence because your team is too polite - or too scared - to raise them

**Use for:** One line per post that makes the reader feel seen. Place it after the scene, before the structural diagnosis.

### Layer 4: Narrative loops (the recurring patterns)

- **The reorg loop:** New strategy -> reorg -> 6 months of confusion -> things stabilise -> new strategy -> reorg again
- **The consulting loop:** Hire a firm -> get a 200-slide deck -> implement 20% -> declare victory -> same problems 12 months later
- **The tool loop:** Buy a new tool to fix a process problem -> tool doesn't fix it because the process was never defined -> blame the tool -> buy another tool
- **The alignment loop:** Offsite -> alignment -> back to the office -> alignment dissolves by Wednesday -> schedule another offsite
- **The hero loop:** One person holds it together -> they burn out or leave -> panic -> find another hero -> repeat
- **The firefighting loop:** No planning -> everything is urgent -> firefight all day -> no time to plan -> no planning
- **The training loop:** Identify a gap -> buy training -> team attends -> nobody changes behaviour -> identify the same gap next quarter
- **The nod-and-ignore loop:** Set goals -> everyone agrees -> nobody executes -> review meeting -> "we need to be more disciplined" -> set goals again
- **The SOP graveyard loop:** Problem happens -> someone writes an SOP -> SOP goes into a folder -> nobody reads it -> same problem happens -> "why didn't you follow the SOP?" -> "I never look in there"
- **The silent rot loop:** Problem appears -> person doesn't want to bother busy leader -> problem sits -> problem grows -> problem explodes -> leader finds out too late -> "why didn't anyone tell me?"
- **The CEO bottleneck loop:** Decision needed -> team afraid to decide wrong -> escalate to CEO -> CEO overloaded -> decision delayed -> team waits -> nothing moves -> CEO frustrated that "nobody takes ownership"

**Use for:** Posts about patterns. "You've seen this movie before" energy. Loops name the shape of the problem, not just the symptoms. The reader recognises the arc of their own last 18 months.

## Writing Rules for This Tribe

### Tone
- Commanding. Not aggressive, not soft. The tone of someone who's seen this 50 times and knows exactly what's broken.
- Cut softening language. Not "you might want to consider" - instead "fix this first."
- No hedging. "This is what's broken" not "this could potentially be an area of improvement."
- Short dash (-) not em dash.

### Structure
- TLDR up front when the post is long
- Numbered sections for sequential content
- One idea per paragraph
- Specific tactics over generic advice
- Avoid over-explaining

### What to avoid
- "Leadership is about..." (generic, Level 1)
- Inspirational quotes from famous people (Noise quadrant)
- Anything that could be a Diary of a CEO episode (Steven Bartlett test)
- "In my experience, organisations often..." (consultant voice, Level 2-3)
- Explaining technical vocabulary to outsiders (kills the credibility signal)
- Combining multiple scenes in one post (dilutes recognition)
- Leading with technical vocabulary as the hook (doesn't fire recognition for this tribe)
- Generic management concepts as hooks: "alignment," "culture," "communication" (leak everywhere)

### What to maximise
- Scenes from the bank as hooks (the scene IS the tribal signal)
- The gap between what they call the problem and what the problem actually is
- One cultural texture line per post - placed for emotional impact
- Narrative loops that name the shape of the pattern, not just the symptom
- Your Director's Cut - your opinion earned from being in the room
- The reframe: "It sounds like X. It's not. It's Y." This is the move from recognition to authority.
